# More Than a Survivor: Why National Cancer Survivor Month Matters to All of Us

## You Made It. But the Journey Does Not End There.

Think about someone you love.

A parent. A best friend. A neighbour. A coworker whose name makes you smile.

Now imagine getting a phone call that changes everything, a cancer diagnosis. Weeks of fear, appointments, treatments, and uncertainty. Then, one day, a doctor says the words you have been holding your breath to hear: ***the cancer is gone***, or *you are in remission*, or *we caught it in time*.

That moment is extraordinary. It is worth celebrating with everything you have.

But here is something that does not get talked about nearly enough: surviving cancer is not the finish line. For millions of people, it is the beginning of a completely new and often very difficult chapter.

June is **National Cancer Survivor Month** — and this year, we at the Alliance for CancerCare Equity (ACCE) want to go beyond the ribbon and the applause. We want to talk honestly about what survival really looks like, who gets left out of the celebration, and why your support matters more than ever.

## What Is National Cancer Survivor Month?

Every June, communities around the world recognise and honour people living with and beyond cancer. The movement is anchored by **National Cancer Survivors Day**, which in 2026 takes place on **June 7**.

The definition of a cancer survivor is broader than most people realize. According to the National Cancer Survivors Day Foundation, anyone living with a history of cancer, from the moment of diagnosis through the rest of their life — is a survivor. That includes people actively in treatment, in remission, or who have been cancer-free for decades.

The numbers reflect just how many people this touches:

- As of 2025, there are an estimated **18.6 million cancer survivors** in the United States alone, roughly 5.4% of the entire population.
- In Canada, more than **1.5 million people** are living with and beyond cancer, a number that has grown significantly over the past decade as treatments improve and more lives are saved.
- Globally, survival rates continue to rise. Today, approximately **70% of cancer survivors** have lived five or more years after their diagnosis, compared to just 49% in the 1970s.

These numbers are cause for hope. They are also a call to action. Because behind every statistic is a real person navigating a life that has been permanently changed.

## The Hidden Battles After "You're Cancer-Free"

When someone finishes treatment, people around them often breathe a collective sigh of relief. The hard part is over, right?

Not always.

Many survivors describe a strange and lonely experience after their treatment ends. The appointments that once filled every week suddenly stop. The support system that rallied around them during treatment quietly disperses. And the survivor is left to figure out: *Who am I now? What does normal even mean anymore?*

This is sometimes called the **"new normal"** — and it comes with real, ongoing challenges that rarely make it into the headlines.

**Emotionally**, one in three cancer patients experiences depression or anxiety, and these feelings often intensify after treatment ends, not during it. The fear that cancer will return, called fear of recurrence, is one of the most commonly reported concerns among survivors, and it can be paralyzing.

**Physically**, many survivors live with long-term side effects from treatment: fatigue, nerve damage, hormonal changes, chronic pain, or cognitive difficulties sometimes called "chemo brain." These are not complaints, they are real medical consequences that deserve real support.

**Financially**, the burden can follow survivors for years. According to the **AACR Cancer Progress Report 2025**, more than **56% of people with cancer experience catastrophic health expenditures**. In Canada, the average cancer patient faces nearly $33,000 in costs over the course of their illness. For those in lower-income households, the impact is even sharper, with many forced to cut essential spending, take on debt, or leave employment altogether.

And in countries like Ghana, where ACCE works alongside patients every day, these barriers are even more extreme. Most patients bear the costs of their diagnosis entirely out of pocket, without insurance, and often without a support system to help them navigate a complex system.

Survival should not depend on your bank account. It should not depend on where you were born.

## Why We Celebrate — And Why It Matters to You

You might be reading this and thinking: *I have never had cancer. What does this have to do with me?*

It has everything to do with you.

Cancer does not stay within the edges of one person's life. It ripples outward — to families, friends, workplaces, and communities. It is estimated that **42% of all people in Canada will receive a cancer diagnosis in their lifetime**. That means it is not a matter of if cancer will touch your life, but when.

National Cancer Survivor Month is a reminder that we are all in this together. It is a moment to:

- **Acknowledge** the people in your life who have faced cancer and come out the other side, and the ones still fighting.
- **Learn** about the ongoing challenges survivors face, so you can show up for them in meaningful ways.
- **Advocate** for a world where every survivor, regardless of income or geography, has access to the follow-up care, mental health support, and financial resources they need.
- **Act**, because awareness without action is not enough.

## How ACCE Supports Survivors — and How You Can Too

At ACCE, our mission is equity in cancer care from prevention through survivorship. We believe that the journey does not end at remission, and that every patient deserves support not just to survive, but to truly live.

We do this by:

- **Funding** the full spectrum of cancer treatments, including radiotherapy, surgery, chemotherapy, and associated costs, for patients in Ghana who lack the financial means to pay for their treatment.
- **Assisting** with the costs of essential, yet unfunded, medications for patients in Canada.
- **Providing** financial support for cancer treatments to uninsured and underinsured patients in Canada.
- **Advocating for access**, because we know that in both Canada and Ghana, where you live and how much you earn still shapes your chances of surviving cancer.

But we cannot do it alone. Every single donation, no matter the size, funds real treatment for a real person. It means a mother gets the medication she needs. It means a young person does not have to choose between rent and a follow-up scan. It means someone's survival story gets written at all.
