# 5 Big Questions Cancer Research Is Still Trying to Answer

When someone receives a cancer diagnosis, countless questions are probably running through their mind.

Cancer research has come a long way. Advances in prevention, early detection, and treatment have helped save millions of lives around the world. Yet despite decades of scientific progress, cancer remains one of the leading causes of death globally and many important questions still do not have clear answers.

Here are five of the biggest questions researchers continue to explore in the fight against cancer.

## 1. What causes cancer?

This is one of the most common questions that people ask from time to time. While very few cancers have a single known cause, most cancers seem to be caused by a complex mix of risk factors. In some cases, cancer can develop in people who appear to have no obvious risk factors at all.

Some risks are beyond our control, including aging, certain inherited genetic changes and family history.

Other risk factors are linked to lifestyle and environment, including:

- Smoking tobacco
- Excessive sun exposure without protection
- Excess body weight
- Poor diet
- Lack of physical activity
- Alcohol consumption
- Exposure to harmful chemicals at home or work
- Certain infections, including HPV and hepatitis viruses

Although not all cancers can be prevented, taking small but important steps toward a healthier lifestyle can help reduce risk.

## 2. How does cancer grow and spread?

The human body is made up of trillions of cells grouped together to form tissues and organs. Genes inside each cell tell it when to grow, work, divide and die. Sometimes the instructions get mixed up, causing these cells to grow and divide abnormally. Over time, these cells may form a lump in the body called a [tumour](https://my.clevelandclinic.org/health/diseases/21881-tumor).

Not all tumours are cancerous. [Benign tumours](https://my.clevelandclinic.org/health/diseases/21881-tumor) do not spread to other parts of the body and often do not return after removal. Cancerous, or malignant, tumours can grow into nearby tissues and spread throughout the body.

As tumours grow, cancer cells can signal the body to create new blood vessels that supply oxygen and nutrients, helping the cancer continue to grow. Cancer cells may also break away from the original tumour and travel through the blood or lymphatic system to other parts of the body, a process known as [metastasis](https://my.clevelandclinic.org/health/diseases/22213-metastasis-metastatic-cancer).

## 3. How does cancer treatment work?

There are several types of treatment for cancer. The main cancer treatments are surgery, [chemotherapy](https://my.clevelandclinic.org/health/treatments/16859-chemotherapy) and [radiation therapy](https://cancer.ca/en/treatments/treatment-types/radiation-therapy). Other treatments include hormone therapy, targeted therapy, immunotherapy and stem cell transplant.

The type of treatment a person will have depends on factors, including the type of cancer and the stage, the person's age and personal preferences. Sometimes people who have the same cancer will be given different treatments. Some will undergo only one treatment, while others will have a combination such as surgery with chemotherapy or radiation therapy or both.

The genetic mutations that cancer cells acquire over time mean that the cells change the way they behave, leading to cancer cells developing resistance to a treatment over time. If that happens, the patient will have to be put on a different treatment.

## 4. How does cancer become life-threatening?

This is one of the most common questions patients ask. [Scientists](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5902718/) say cancer rarely kills by mechanically blocking an organ. Rarely does a patient die of liver failure or kidney failure because the cancer has effaced the organ.

As patients approach a total tumor load of about a kilogram of tumor, which is approximately one trillion cells, this is not compatible with life. The way cancer becomes life-threatening varies from person to person. In many cases, it affects the body's ability to function properly by weakening vital systems, disrupting normal organ function, or spreading extensively throughout the body.

## 5. Will cancer ever be cured?

It is unlikely that there will ever be a single medicine that can cure all cancers. However, scientists believe that research can help reach a day when no life is cut short by cancer.
