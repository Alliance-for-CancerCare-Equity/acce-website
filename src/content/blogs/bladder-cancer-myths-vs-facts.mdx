# Bladder Cancer: What You Don't Know Could Cost You

*Myths Busted. Facts That Matter.*

Imagine noticing blood in your urine one morning. It doesn't hurt. It disappears the next day. You think, maybe it was nothing. Maybe it was something you ate. And you move on.

But for many, that "nothing" moment was actually the first and most important warning sign of bladder cancer.

Bladder cancer is the 5th most common cancer in Canada, with over 80,000 Canadians currently living with the disease. Approximately 37 Canadians are diagnosed every single day. Yet it remains one of the least talked about cancers, partly because of embarrassment, and partly because of myths that stop people from taking symptoms seriously.

Today, we are busting the 5 most common myths about bladder cancer, because what you don't know could cost you, or someone you love, precious time.

## Myth 1: "Only older men get bladder cancer."

**Fact: Anyone can get bladder cancer, at any age.**

Yes, the risk of bladder cancer increases with age, and men historically face a higher incidence. But [recent statistics](https://bladdercancercanada.org/en/bladder-cancer/bladder-cancer-statistics-and-risk-factors/) show a rising number of women being diagnosed, and because symptoms can be mistaken for urinary tract infections or gynecological issues, women are often diagnosed at a more advanced stage. This matters. When the disease is caught late, it is much harder and more expensive to treat. No one should be dismissed because they do not "fit the profile."

## Myth 2: "If there's no pain, there's no problem."

**Fact: The most common symptom of bladder cancer is painless.**

This is the myth that can cost lives. The most common symptom of bladder cancer is painless blood in the urine, called [hematuria](https://bladdercancercanada.org/en/bladder-cancer/see-red/), seen in approximately 85% of patients with the disease. People see the blood, feel no pain, and assume it will go away. Sometimes it does, temporarily. But ignoring it can allow a treatable cancer to become a life-threatening one. If you ever see blood in your urine, even once, even without pain, please see a doctor. Do not wait.

## Myth 3: "Bladder cancer isn't that serious. It's easy to treat."

**Fact: Bladder cancer is the most expensive cancer to treat per patient, and it comes back.**

Bladder cancer is the most expensive cancer to treat on a per patient basis, and [Non-Muscle Invasive Bladder Cancer](https://bladdercancercanada.org/en/bladder-cancer/types-of-bladder-cancer/non-muscle-invasive-bladder-cancer/) has a 60–70% recurrence rate. That means even after successful treatment, patients must undergo regular surveillance for years. For those without financial resources, this ongoing treatment burden can be devastating. This is exactly why the work of organizations like Alliance for CancerCare Equity (ACCE) matters so deeply.

## Myth 4: "Bladder cancer only happens to smokers."

**Fact: Smoking is the biggest risk factor, but it's not the only one.**

Smoking is the most common risk factor associated with bladder cancer, with smokers facing a doubled diagnosis rate compared to non-smokers. But exposure to certain workplace chemicals, notably those involved in dye making and aromatic amines, is also linked to an increased risk. This means factory workers, painters, hairdressers, and others with chemical exposure may also be at elevated risk, even if they've never smoked a day in their life. Risk doesn't always look like what we expect.

## Myth 5: "There's nothing I can do to protect myself."

**Fact: There is more within your control than you think.**

Quitting smoking is the single most impactful step a person can take to reduce their risk. Staying well-hydrated, limiting exposure to harmful chemicals, and, most importantly, knowing the symptoms and acting quickly if something feels wrong, are all actions that can lead to earlier diagnosis and better outcomes. When bladder cancer is detected early, treatment is highly effective and survival rates can reach up to 90%. That number drops significantly with late-stage diagnosis. Early action isn't just helpful, it's life-saving.

## The Reality No One Talks About: The Cost of Getting Sick

Here is something the myths don't cover: even when someone does everything right, they notice symptoms early, they see a doctor, they get diagnosed, the battle isn't over. For many patients in underserved communities in Canada and Ghana, the next obstacle is the one that feels impossible: paying for treatment.

At the ACCE, we believe that no one should be denied cancer care because of where they live or how much they earn. Yet every day, patients are forced to choose between food, shelter, and lifesaving care.

One patient we supported said it best:

> "ACCE's donors paid for my surgery and chemotherapy when I had nothing. They gave me hope when I had none."

That is why we do this work. And that is why your support matters.
